Best ChatGPT Prompts for Small Business Owners (50+ Proven Templates)

By Zachary Aronwald | February 15, 2026

You've heard ChatGPT can save you hours every week. You've opened it a dozen times. And then… you stare at the blank prompt box, type something vague like "write me a social media post," and get back something generic and useless.

Sound familiar?

The problem isn't ChatGPT. It's that most people don't know how to talk to it. A great prompt is the difference between "meh" content you can't use and "wow, this is better than I'd write myself" output.

This post gives you 50+ battle-tested ChatGPT prompts specifically designed for small business owners. Copy, paste, customize, and watch your productivity skyrocket.

Why Good Prompts Matter

ChatGPT is like a brilliant intern with no context. It can do amazing work—if you give it clear instructions.

A bad prompt:

"Write a Facebook post about my business."

A good prompt:

"Write a 3-sentence Facebook post for a local dog grooming business. Target: busy pet parents in suburban Austin. Tone: warm and playful. Angle: introduce our new 'spa day' package that includes nail trim, bath, and bandana. Include a call-to-action to book online. Keep it under 50 words."

The second one gives ChatGPT everything it needs to write something you can actually use.

The Anatomy of a Great Business Prompt

Every high-performing prompt includes these elements:

  1. Role: "You're a [copywriter/strategist/analyst] for [type of business]"
  2. Task: "Write a [specific deliverable]"
  3. Context: "For [audience], about [topic]"
  4. Constraints: "Tone: [X]. Length: [Y]. Format: [Z]."
  5. Goal: "The purpose is to [convert/inform/engage]"

Use this framework, and your results instantly improve.

50+ Copy-Paste Prompts by Category

Marketing & Copywriting

1. Social Media Post Generator

You're a social media manager for a [your business type]. Write a [platform] post about [topic/product/service]. Target audience: [describe]. Tone: [professional/casual/funny]. Include a hook in the first sentence, value in the middle, and a clear call-to-action. Keep it under [X] words.

2. Email Subject Line Ideas

Generate 10 email subject lines for a [your business type] sending a [type of email: newsletter/promo/announcement]. The email is about [topic]. Target audience: [describe]. Make them curiosity-driven, not clickbait. Include emoji options.

3. Google/Facebook Ad Copy

Write 3 versions of ad copy for a [your business type] promoting [product/service]. Target: [audience]. Each version should include: headline (30 characters max), description (90 characters max), and call-to-action. Focus on benefits, not features.

4. Landing Page Headline

Write 5 landing page headlines for [product/service] aimed at [target audience]. The main benefit is [X]. Tone: [persuasive but not salesy]. Use the format: [benefit] + [timeframe/ease] + [outcome].

5. Blog Post Outline

Create a detailed blog post outline for "[blog post title]". Target keyword: [X]. Include: meta description (160 characters), 5-7 H2 headers, 2-3 bullet points under each header, and a conclusion with a call-to-action. Optimize for SEO and readability.

Sales & Customer Communication

6. Follow-Up Email After Quote

Write a follow-up email to a potential client who received a project quote [X days] ago. Tone: professional but warm. Acknowledge they're busy, ask if they have questions, and offer a quick call. Keep it under 100 words. Don't sound desperate.

7. Cold Outreach Email

Write a cold outreach email to [type of business/role] offering [your service]. Personalize it using this info: [company name, industry, pain point you noticed]. Tone: helpful, not salesy. Structure: compliment + problem you noticed + how you can help + low-pressure ask. 75 words max.

8. Objection Handler

A potential customer said: "[common objection]". Write a 2-3 sentence response that acknowledges their concern, provides social proof or a guarantee, and invites them to take a small next step. Tone: empathetic and confident.

9. Testimonial Request

Write an email asking a happy client for a testimonial. Mention the specific project/result: [describe]. Make it easy by giving them a 3-question template: What was your problem? How did we help? What changed? Tone: grateful but not groveling.

10. Pricing Explanation

A customer asked why your [product/service] costs [price]. Write a 4-sentence explanation that breaks down the value, compares it to alternatives, and reframes the investment. Avoid being defensive.

Operations & Productivity

11. Standard Operating Procedure (SOP)

Create a step-by-step SOP for [task/process in your business]. Target user: [employee/VA/yourself]. Format: numbered list with substeps. Include: what to do, how to do it, where to find resources, and how to check quality.

12. Meeting Agenda

Generate a meeting agenda for a [type of meeting: client kickoff/team standup/strategy session]. Duration: [X] minutes. Include: objective, 4-6 discussion topics with time allocations, and key decisions needed. Leave 5 minutes for wrap-up.

13. Process Improvement Brainstorm

I run a [type of business]. Currently, [describe a bottleneck or repetitive task]. Suggest 5 ways to streamline or automate this using low-cost tools. Focus on practical, implementable ideas.

14. Task Prioritization

I have these 10 tasks: [list them]. My goal this week is [X]. Which 3 should I focus on first based on impact and urgency? Explain your reasoning in one sentence per task.

15. Decision Framework

I'm trying to decide between [Option A] and [Option B] for [context: hiring, software, strategy]. Here are the pros and cons: [list them]. Based on my goal of [X], which should I choose and why?

Customer Service

16. Refund Response (Approved)

Write a refund approval email for a customer who [reason for refund]. Tone: professional, gracious, and solution-focused. Include: acknowledgment, refund confirmation, timeline, and an invitation to return in the future. Keep it warm, not robotic.

17. Complaint Response

A customer complained about [issue]. Write a response that: acknowledges their frustration, takes responsibility (if applicable), explains what went wrong, and offers a concrete solution or compensation. Tone: empathetic and accountable. Under 150 words.

18. FAQ Answers

Write a friendly, clear answer to this frequently asked question: "[customer question]". Target audience: [describe]. Tone: helpful and conversational. Structure: direct answer + brief explanation + link to more info if needed. 50-75 words.

19. "Sorry We Messed Up" Email

We made a mistake: [describe what went wrong]. Write an apology email to affected customers. Tone: sincere, not defensive. Include: what happened, how we're fixing it, what we're doing to prevent it, and a goodwill gesture if appropriate.

20. Out-of-Stock Notification

A customer ordered [product] but it's temporarily out of stock. Write an email explaining the situation, offering alternatives or a wait option, and providing a discount code as an apology. Tone: apologetic but optimistic. Under 100 words.

Strategy & Planning

21. Competitor Analysis Summary

I'm researching [competitor name]. Based on their website, social media, and customer reviews, summarize: their positioning, unique value prop, pricing strategy, strengths, and weaknesses. Format: bullet points. Suggest one thing we should do differently.

22. Content Calendar

Create a 4-week content calendar for [platform] for a [your business type]. Topics should align with [your goal: awareness/engagement/sales]. Include: post topic, format (carousel/video/text), and hook for each. 3 posts per week.

23. New Product/Service Idea Validator

I'm considering launching [product/service]. Target audience: [describe]. Price point: [X]. Assess: market demand, competition level, resources needed, and risks. Give me a go/no-go recommendation with reasoning.

24. Year-End Review Questions

Generate 10 reflection questions for a small business owner to evaluate the past year. Focus on: revenue, customer satisfaction, team growth, lessons learned, and opportunities. Keep questions actionable, not just philosophical.

25. Goal-Setting Framework

I want to [achieve X goal] in [timeframe]. Break this into: 1 overarching objective, 3 key results (measurable), and 5 action steps per key result. Use the SMART framework (Specific, Measurable, Achievable, Relevant, Time-bound).

How to Customize These Prompts

Every prompt here is a template. To make it work for your business:

  1. Replace bracketed placeholders with your specifics
  2. Add context (industry, audience, tone, constraints)
  3. Iterate if the first output isn't perfect—ask ChatGPT to revise
  4. Save your best prompts in a doc or tool like Notion for reuse

For example, if you're a real estate agent, transform the cold outreach prompt into:

"Write a cold outreach email to homeowners in [neighborhood] who listed their home 6+ months ago but didn't sell. Offer a free market analysis and staging tips. Tone: helpful, not pushy. Structure: acknowledge their situation + insight into current market + offer help + easy next step. 75 words max."

Advanced Tip: Chain Prompts Together

Don't stop at one prompt. Layer them:

  1. "Generate 10 blog post ideas for [audience] about [topic]"
  2. (Pick one) "Create a detailed outline for blog post idea #3"
  3. (Review outline) "Write the introduction section. Make it engaging and lead with a surprising stat."

This "prompt chaining" gives you control over quality at every stage.

Common Prompt Mistakes to Avoid

Mistake 1: Being too vague. "Write something for my business" won't cut it. Be specific.

Mistake 2: Not specifying tone. ChatGPT defaults to "professional but generic." Tell it: casual, authoritative, playful, etc.

Mistake 3: Asking for too much at once. Break big projects into smaller prompts. Instead of "write a full sales page," ask for headlines first, then bullet points, then testimonials, etc.

Mistake 4: Not reviewing the output. Always read and edit. ChatGPT can be wrong, generic, or off-brand.
